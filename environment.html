Your Surroundings Mean More

We often think that we are more adaptable than we really are. We've inhabited every nook and cranny of the Earth, dominated sky, land, and sea, and have come so far. But, in reality, we are more-so creatures of habit than creatures of adventure.

Would your favorite food taste the same if it were eaten in the bathroom? Probably not! Would NBA players be able to play to their potential if instead of hardwood they had a grass court? Probably not! Experiences have their own homes in our mind, built on sights, smells, feelings, and everything else that they come with.

Now, I'm not insinuating that we shouldn't do anything out of the ordinary. Rather, I'm saying that for processes that we repeat often or are important, we should try our best to keep all controllable conditions the same. We do well with familiarity and a lack of surprises. Our brains are built to be wary of changes, because changes can often mean danger.

When it comes to learning, we should avoid switching things up too drastically or allowing ourselves us to be subject to poor conditions. Here's a quick list of what you should keep in mind:

  1. Mood - If you're angry or upset and you can still focus on learning, you might be superhuman. For the rest of us mere mortals, trying to learn while feeling more emotional than normal probably won't work. If you're going through some things, take a break and do something relaxing, whatever that is for you. Try again later.
  2. Position - Some of us learn better sitting. Some, standing. Others, upside-down. Whatever it is, listen to your mind and body and make changes accordingly if they're necessary. Otherwise, keep it consistent. If you want to lay down immediately after sitting (and are a sitting learner), then maybe you're tired and shouldn't be learning at that time.
  3. Time - Personally, I do my best learning early in the day. I eat my figuratives frogs first thing in the morning so that I don't have to worry about it later on. I would not do well if I tried to watch a lecture at 11 pm.
  4. Noise - Most of us like things quiet when we're learning, and any annoying sounds can quickly throw us out of the flowstates we work so hard to get into.

Beyond your physical environment, your mental environment has a big impact on your state of mind. The people you surround yourself with in your day-to-day greatly affect how you see yourself, how you gauge where you are in life, etc. Being surrounded by a bunch of aspirational hard-workers is a very different experience from being surrounded by lazy bums.

Take your environment into careful consideration and make the changes you need to become a better learner.

Source: Learning how to Learn: A course by Coursera and Barbara Oakley
